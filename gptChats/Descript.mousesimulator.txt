## Mechanistic mouse model: algorithmic structure

### 1. Skeleton representation

The mouse body is represented as a **keypoint graph**. The input file has a `[NODES]` section giving 3D coordinates and an `[EDGES]` section giving skeletal connections. The simulator normalizes labels, stores node coordinates, preserves node order, and later uses the graph both for pose generation and rendering. fileciteturn2file0L66-L137

This is a **geometric skeleton**, not a mass-spring, articulated rigid-body, muscle, or joint-torque model. The graph defines visible landmarks; the simulator then procedurally moves those landmarks.

### 2. Per-mouse locomotion state

Each mouse has a low-dimensional trajectory state:

`x, y` = cage-plane position  
`theta` = heading angle  
`v` = speed  
`omega` = yaw rate

This is stored in `TrackState`. fileciteturn2file0L200-L206

The movement update is an “OU-ish” stochastic process: speed and yaw rate are damped and perturbed by Gaussian noise. Then additional steering terms are added: wall repulsion, social target attraction, and avoidance of the other mouse. Finally, the mouse is advanced in the direction of its heading. fileciteturn2file0L241-L296

So the trajectory model is not solving Newtonian mechanics. It is a **stochastic steering model** with smooth motion and bounded speed/yaw dynamics.

### 3. Individual behavior switching

Each mouse also has a behavioral state: running or rearing. The state has a timer, a sampled duration, a gait phase, and parameters controlling the rearing pose. fileciteturn2file0L303-L311

The behavior process is a simple semi-Markov/state-machine rule:

- running lasts uniformly between 2 and 6 seconds,
- rearing lasts uniformly between 0.6 and 1.6 seconds,
- after running, the mouse switches to rearing with probability 0.25, otherwise remains running,
- after rearing, it returns to running. fileciteturn2file0L314-L330

During a social episode, this individual run/rear timer is frozen, so social interaction has priority over individual behavior. fileciteturn2file0L999-L1018

### 4. Running pose model

Running is generated procedurally from the skeleton. The head/nose/ears undergo small sinusoidal jitter in pitch, roll, and yaw. The paws are moved along the local x-axis using a triangular wave, with diagonal paw pairs moving in phase: left front with right hind, and right front with left hind. The gait phase advances according to body speed divided by stride length. fileciteturn2file0L337-L395

This is best interpreted as a **trot-like keypoint animation**, not a contact-dynamics gait model. The code even notes that paw motion uses ground-plane x-offsets and does not enforce strict stance constraints. fileciteturn2file0L24-L26

### 5. Rearing pose model

Rearing uses a sinusoidal temporal envelope `h = sin(pi * u)`, where `u` is normalized time within the rearing bout. The body cluster is rotated around the tail root, the hind paws are fixed on the ground, and the front paws are repositioned by rotating their vectors so their vertical position approaches a target lift. fileciteturn2file0L398-L449

Mechanically, this is a **procedural pose morph**: the mouse rises and returns according to a smooth envelope. It mimics rearing kinematics, but it does not model center of mass, balance, joint limits, torques, or contact forces.

### 6. Collision and wall constraints

The body is approximated by two 2D spheres/circles: a front sphere near the head/nose and a rear sphere near the tail root. Their radii are computed from the keypoint graph. fileciteturn2file0L565-L620

After tentative movement, the simulator applies a position projection:

- sphere centers are kept inside the cage,
- all front/rear sphere pairs between the two mice are separated,
- “dominance” weights decide which mouse moves more when resolving overlap. fileciteturn2file0L623-L698

This is a hard geometric correction layer. It gives non-interpenetration, but it is not a physical collision response with momentum, friction, impulses, or deformation.

### 7. Social interaction episode

Social interaction is a pair-level finite-state machine:

`NONE → APPROACH → HOLD → SEPARATE`

The states are defined explicitly in `SocialMode`, and the `SocialState` stores the current phase, initiator, meeting point, touch frame/time, and minimum nose distance. fileciteturn2file0L701-L730

Episodes are scheduled stochastically with an exponential waiting time based on `social_rate_per_min` plus a cooldown. When an episode begins, the simulator chooses a dominance-weighted initiator, computes a meeting point, and steers the mice toward it. fileciteturn2file0L945-L997

During approach, the initiator moves more directly toward the meeting point, while the responder moves more slowly. When the noses come within a threshold, the system enters hold; after a fixed hold duration, it switches to separate; separation ends when the mice are far enough apart or a timeout occurs. fileciteturn2file0L1027-L1103

So social behavior is not emergent from local forces alone. It is an explicit **scripted interaction primitive** layered over the individual movement model.

### 8. Arbitration hierarchy

Each frame has a clear control stack:

1. update or start social FSM,
2. update individual behavior timers only if not social,
3. determine steering targets,
4. advance both track states,
5. enforce hard collision/wall constraints,
6. generate local poses,
7. transform local skeletons into world coordinates,
8. write 3D keypoints, ethogram labels, 2D camera projections, and optional videos. fileciteturn2file0L974-L1249

That hierarchy is the core “mechanism” of the simulator: stochastic motion + state-machine behavior + procedural poses + geometric constraints + camera projection.

## Is “mechanistic” a reasonable term?

**Yes, but with an important qualifier.**

“Mechanistic” is reasonable if you mean:

> the simulator generates behavior from explicit internal rules, state variables, transition logic, geometric constraints, and procedural pose transformations.

In that sense, it is mechanistic as opposed to purely data-driven, black-box, or statistical resampling. The behavior is produced by identifiable mechanisms: state machines, steering equations, collision projection, pose-generation rules, and camera models.

But “mechanistic” can be misleading in a biological or physical modeling context. A reader might assume the model contains real mechanisms of mouse locomotion: musculoskeletal anatomy, joint torques, contact mechanics, neural control, balance, friction, inertia, or learned behavioral policies. This program does **not** do that. It is mechanistic at the **algorithmic/rule level**, not at the **biophysical or causal-neuroethological level**.

A precise phrase would be:

> **a rule-based stochastic kinematic mouse simulator**

or, longer:

> **a hierarchical rule-based stochastic kinematic simulator with finite-state behavioral control and geometric collision constraints.**

## Better alternative terms

The best label depends on the audience.

| Term | Accuracy | Comment |
|---|---:|---|
| **Rule-based stochastic kinematic simulator** | Very high | Probably the most accurate concise description. |
| **Procedural behavioral animation model** | High | Good if the emphasis is synthetic video/keypoint generation. |
| **Agent-based kinematic simulation** | High | Good because there are two independently controlled mice. |
| **Hybrid dynamical system** | High, more formal | Continuous motion plus discrete behavior modes. |
| **Finite-state behavioral simulator** | Medium-high | Captures run/rear/social switching, but underdescribes geometry and motion. |
| **Mechanistic simulator** | Medium | Acceptable, but too broad unless qualified. |
| **Biomechanical model** | Low | Not appropriate; no physical body dynamics or joint mechanics. |
| **Physics-based model** | Low | Collision projection exists, but not true physics simulation. |
| **Ethological model** | Medium-low | It imitates ethological categories, but the behaviors are scripted/heuristic. |
| **Generative synthetic-data model** | High for purpose | Accurate if the purpose is generating labeled keypoint/video data. |

My recommended naming would be:

> **Hierarchical stochastic kinematic mouse simulator**

or, if you want to emphasize interpretability:

> **Mechanistic rule-based kinematic mouse simulator**

That preserves the useful sense of “mechanistic” while avoiding the stronger implication that it is a true biomechanical or biological mechanism model.

User: What does FSM stand for?

Assistant: **FSM** stands for **Finite-State Machine**.

In this simulator, it means the mouse or interaction behavior is modeled as a small set of discrete states with rules for moving between them.

For example, the social interaction FSM is:

`NONE → APPROACH → HOLD → SEPARATE → NONE`

Each state has its own control logic, and transitions occur when conditions are met, such as nose distance becoming small enough, a hold timer expiring, or separation distance being reached.
